A recent expansion project near the Yamaska River hit an unexpected pocket of saturated silts at just four meters depth—right where the proposed spread footings were supposed to anchor into competent till. The geotechnical team on that Granby site brought in CPT testing to confirm the soft zone extent, then used vertical electrical sounding to map the clay-sand interface across the entire parcel. Granby’s subsurface combines glacial Lake Vermont deposits with Champlain Sea clays, which means resistivity contrasts can shift dramatically over short distances. Electrical resistivity testing reads these contrasts by injecting current into the ground and measuring how the formation responds. The resulting profiles reveal stratigraphy, water saturation, and potential contamination plumes without turning a single shovelful of soil. For engineers working on Granby’s eastern industrial corridor, where buried valleys complicate foundation design, resistivity data fills the gap between sparse boreholes and the continuous subsurface picture that structural loads actually care about.

Electrical resistivity turns the invisible subsurface into a measurable cross-section—critical in Granby where marine clay lenses hide between competent till layers.

Methodology applied in Granby Quebec

ASTM D6431 sets the standard for field resistivity measurement using multi-electrode arrays, and in Granby the methodology proves especially valuable given the contact between resistive glacial tills and conductive marine clays. The Wenner and Schlumberger configurations each serve different depth targets: Wenner excels at identifying horizontal layering within the first 15 meters, while Schlumberger pushes investigation depth beyond 40 meters with fewer electrode repositionings. A typical Granby survey deploys 28 to 56 electrodes along a profile line, collecting apparent resistivity values that undergo inversion processing to generate true resistivity cross-sections. These cross-sections help engineers distinguish between dry granular soils that can support footings and saturated fine-grained zones requiring ground improvement. Because Granby sits within the Appalachian foothills transition, resistivity tomography frequently picks up the irregular bedrock surface where seismic methods struggle with velocity inversions. The technique also supports environmental due diligence by detecting leachate migration from former landfill cells along Rue Cowie.

Risks and considerations in Granby Quebec

Granby’s population has grown past 66,000, pushing residential subdivisions into areas where the Champlain Sea left thick, highly sensitive clay deposits. These clays exhibit resistivity values below 10 ohm-m, making them clearly identifiable in VES soundings—but if overlooked during the planning phase, they introduce differential settlement risks that can crack foundation walls within the first freeze-thaw cycle. The 2020 Montérégie seismic event, though moderate at magnitude 3.9, reminded local engineers that the Western Quebec Seismic Zone extends into this region. Saturated clays under seismic loading can lose strength abruptly, and resistivity profiling maps exactly where those layers sit relative to proposed deep excavations. Groundwater perched within granular lenses atop the clay aquitard creates another hazard: resistivity lows flag the saturated zones where dewatering volumes may exceed initial estimates by a factor of three or more.

2D Electrical Resistivity Tomography

Multi-electrode profiling along transects up to 225 meters for mapping lateral and vertical resistivity variations. Applied routinely for bedrock depth determination, groundwater table delineation, and detection of buried channel fills across Granby’s glaciolacustrine terrain.

Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES)

Frequently asked questions

What depth can a resistivity survey reach in Granby soil conditions?

Depth penetration depends on array length and ground resistivity. With a 112-meter Wenner array in typical Granby clay-over-till conditions, investigation depth reaches approximately 20 to 25 meters. Schlumberger arrays with maximum current electrode spacing of 200 meters can resolve bedrock features beyond 60 meters depth where the overburden resistivity permits adequate current flow.

How much does an electrical resistivity survey cost in Granby?

Survey pricing ranges from CA$810 to CA$1.330 depending on profile length, number of electrode positions, and whether 2D tomography or single-point VES is required. Projects needing multiple intersecting lines or 3D inversion processing fall toward the upper end of this range.

Can resistivity distinguish between Champlain Sea clay and glacial till in Granby?

Yes, the contrast is typically strong. Champlain Sea clays in the Granby area register resistivity values between 5 and 20 ohm-m due to their high pore-water salinity and fine grain size, while the underlying glacial till containing sand and gravel lenses measures above 60 ohm-m. This 3x to 12x contrast produces a sharp interface in inverted resistivity sections.

What site preparation is needed before the resistivity crew arrives?

The survey line should be cleared of heavy brush and marked with stakes at electrode positions. Buried metallic utilities within 5 meters of the line can distort readings, so utility locates should be completed beforehand. Frozen ground increases contact resistance significantly; spring through fall surveys yield the most reliable data in Granby’s climate.
