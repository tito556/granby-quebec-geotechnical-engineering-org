Granby sits on a geological transition where the St. Lawrence Lowlands meet the Appalachian foothills. That means one site might have competent limestone while another, just a kilometer away, is underlain by fractured shale or buried glacial till channels. The water table in the Yamaska River valley fluctuates seasonally by more than two meters, which alters seismic velocities enough to fool a basic refraction survey. Our team has worked through these conditions on projects ranging from industrial expansions in the Haut-Richelieu industrial park to residential developments near Lac Boivin. A seismic refraction profile paired with MASW surface wave analysis gives us shear-wave velocity profiles that feed directly into NBCC site classification, while also flagging voids or dissolution features that are common in the Ordovician carbonate units north of Autoroute 10.

Tomographic inversion resolves gradational bedrock weathering that layered refraction models miss by 20-30% in Granby's Champlain Sea clay overburden.

Methodology applied in Granby Quebec

The underlying geology here is dominated by the Nicolet Formation—interbedded shale and siltstone with limestone lenses—overlain by up to 15 meters of Champlain Sea clays and till. Seismic velocities in the shale typically run 2,800 to 3,400 m/s when competent, but drop below 1,900 m/s where the rock is weathered or sheared along the Yamaska fault zone. We deploy 24- to 48-channel seismographs with geophone spacing as tight as 2 meters to resolve these lateral changes. In Granby's industrial park areas, where fill thickness varies from 2 to 8 meters, we often combine tomography with in-situ permeability testing to assess both rippability and water inflow risk during excavation. The refraction data are processed using tomographic inversion algorithms that honor curved ray paths, not just layered models. This matters because weathered rock profiles here are gradational, not sharp, and a delay-time interpretation would underestimate the transition zone thickness by 20-30%.

Risks and considerations in Granby Quebec

We run a 48-channel Geometrics Geode system with 4.5 Hz vertical geophones and a 20-lb sledgehammer source for most Granby sites. On pavement, we bolt geophones to the asphalt with quick-set plaster. On soft clay near the Yamaska River, we use long-spike geophones to maintain coupling above 90% coherence across the spread. The biggest operational risk here is cultural noise: Autoroute 10 generates low-frequency rumble that contaminates frequencies below 12 Hz, and the industrial park has compressors and heavy truck traffic during business hours. We mitigate this by stacking 7-10 impacts per shot point and applying bandpass filtering with corner frequencies at 15 Hz and 400 Hz. A deep excavation project without a verified bedrock profile is gambling with excavation costs—we have seen contractors switch from mechanical ripping to blasting mid-project because the pre-bid seismic data was inadequate.

Our services

We deliver seismic tomography as part of integrated site characterization programs. Each survey is designed around the specific question: bedrock depth, rippability, void detection, or seismic site class.

P-wave refraction tomography

Full tomographic inversion of first-arrival traveltimes. Delivers 2D velocity cross-sections for bedrock mapping, rippability assessment, and detection of low-velocity zones associated with faulting or karst features in the Nicolet Formation.

MASW and Vs30 profiling

Multichannel analysis of surface waves for shear-wave velocity sounding. Provides Vs30 values and velocity profiles for NBCC seismic site classification, liquefaction screening, and dynamic soil-structure interaction inputs.

Combined refraction and resistivity

Co-located seismic and electrical resistivity lines on the same spread. The resistivity data discriminates clay from silt where seismic velocities overlap, which is critical in Champlain Sea deposits where both materials can show 1,600-2,200 m/s P-wave velocities.

Quick answers

How deep can seismic tomography see in Granby's soil conditions?

With a sledgehammer source and 48-channel spread at 5-meter spacing, we typically reach 30 to 50 meters depth in Granby's Champlain Sea clay and till. The actual depth depends on the velocity contrast between overburden and bedrock: in the industrial park where limestone lies at 8-12 meters, penetration is excellent. In areas with thick saturated clay near the Yamaska, attenuation limits depth to the lower end of the range. For deeper targets (50-120 m), we deploy an accelerated weight drop or use longer spreads.

What is the typical cost for seismic refraction in the Granby area?

For a standard seismic refraction survey in Granby, costs range from CA$3,740 to CA$6,330 depending on line length, number of spreads, and whether MASW is added for Vs30 determination. A single 48-channel line of 115 meters with both P-wave refraction and MASW typically falls in the middle of that range. Site access, pavement coupling, and noise mitigation requirements (near Autoroute 10) can shift costs toward the upper end.

Can seismic tomography detect voids or karst in the limestone under Granby?

Yes, but with caveats. Seismic refraction tomography can detect air-filled voids larger than roughly 20% of the overburden thickness when they produce a measurable velocity anomaly. In the Ordovician limestone north of Autoroute 10, we have identified dissolution features as low-velocity zones below 2,200 m/s within otherwise competent rock at 3,200 m/s. However, refraction is not the best tool for small voids—we recommend combining it with electrical resistivity tomography for karst investigations, as resistivity is more sensitive to water-filled cavities.
